<div class="abstract">
<p>Alberta's population grew 30.5% between 2012 and 2025, adding over 1.1 million people. Over the same period, provincial education spending rose 83% in nominal terms — nearly three times faster. This analysis traces that divergence using Statistics Canada population data and Alberta Budget Fiscal Plans across five budget years.</p>
</div>

## Alberta's Population Surge

Alberta's headcount rose from 3,822,425 in Q1 2012 to 4,988,181 in Q1 2025 — an increase of 1,165,756 people, or roughly 30.5% over 13 years.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Alberta Population (Q1 2012) | 3,822,425 |
| Alberta Population (Q1 2025) | 4,988,181 |
| Total Growth | +1,165,756 (~30.5%) |
| Avg. Quarterly Growth Rate | 0.514% |
| Peak Quarterly Growth Rate | 1.356% *(Q4 2023)* |
| Lowest Quarterly Growth Rate | 0.046% *(Q3 2020 — COVID-19)* |
| Alberta's National Share (1951) | 6.7% |
| Alberta's National Share (2025) | 12.1% |

Growth was not uniform. The province tracked a steady long-run average of about 0.51% per quarter, fell to near-zero during the COVID-19 shock in 2020, and then accelerated sharply — peaking at 1.356% per quarter in late 2023, one of the strongest growth quarters on record.

![Quarterly Growth Rate](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/quarterly_growth_rate.png)

Alberta's share of the national population rose from 6.7% in 1951 to 12.1% in 2025 — nearly doubling the province's demographic weight within Canada.

![Alberta's Share of Canada's Total Population](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/alberta_population_share.png)

## Education Spending in Numbers

Five budget years were examined: 2012-13, 2013-14, 2023-24, 2024-25, and 2025-26. All figures are nominal operating expenses in millions of Canadian dollars.

| Budget Year | K-12 ($M) | Post-Secondary ($M) | Total ($M) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2012-13 | 6,179 | 2,856 | 9,035 |
| 2013-14 | 6,210 | 2,682 | 8,892 |
| 2023-24 | 8,836 | 5,604 | 14,440 |
| 2024-25 | 9,252 | 6,305 | 15,557 |
| 2025-26 | 9,883 | 6,635 | 16,518 |

From 2012-13 to 2025-26 in nominal terms: K-12 rose 59.9%, post-secondary rose 132.3%, and total education spending rose 82.8%.

The most striking feature of the data is the step-change between the two early budgets (nearly flat at ~$9B) and the 2023 budget ($14.4B) — a 62% jump across a decade for which annual data is unavailable.

### The 13-Year Growth Gap

![Education Spending Growth: 2012 → 2025](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/infographic_growth_2012_vs_2025.png)

Post-secondary spending grew from $2,856M to $6,635M — more than doubling. K-12 grew from $6,179M to $9,883M, a 60% increase. The two sectors did not grow at the same pace: post-secondary's growth rate was more than twice that of K-12.

### Composition Shift

In 2012, post-secondary represented roughly 32% of total education spending. By 2025, that share had grown to approximately 40%. This is a meaningful structural shift — not just a uniform scale-up of both sectors, but a sustained policy direction of growing university and college funding faster than the school system.

![K-12 vs Post-Secondary Share by Budget Year](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/infographic_donut_composition.png)

![K-12 vs Post-Secondary (Side-by-Side)](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/infographic_k12_vs_postsec.png)

## Did Spending Keep Pace With Population?

The core question: given that Alberta added over a million people in 13 years, did the province invest proportionally more in education?

| Metric | 2012-13 | 2025-26 | Growth |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alberta Population | 3,822,425 | 4,988,181 | **+30.5%** |
| K-12 Operating Expense | $6,179M | $9,883M | **+59.9%** |
| Post-Secondary Operating Expense | $2,856M | $6,635M | **+132.3%** |
| Total Education Expense | $9,035M | $16,518M | **+82.8%** |
| Education Spending per Capita | ~$2,363 | ~$3,312 | **+40.1%** |

The answer is unambiguous: education spending outpaced population growth by a wide margin in every category.

Even K-12 — the slower-growing sector — grew at nearly twice the population growth rate. Total education spending grew at 2.7× the rate of population. Post-secondary grew at 4.3× the population rate.

![Education Spending vs. Population Growth (Growth Rates)](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/integration_growth_rates.png)

The chart above places the population growth rate as a vertical reference line — every spending category extends well past it.

### Indexed Growth

Normalising all metrics to 2012-13 = 100 makes the divergence immediately visible. The data gap between 2014 and 2022 (years for which budget data was not available) is shaded.

![Indexed Growth — All Metrics Normalised to 2012-13 = 100](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/integration_indexed_growth.png)

### Per-Capita Education Spending

Dividing spending by population normalises for the province's growth and isolates the change in per-person investment.

| Metric | 2012-13 | 2025-26 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| K-12 per capita | $1,617 | $1,981 | +22.5% |
| Post-secondary per capita | $747 | $1,330 | +78.0% |
| Total per capita | ~$2,363 | ~$3,312 | +40.1% |

![Per-Capita Education Spending](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/casruta/AB-Population-Increase-vs-Education-Spending/main/plots/integration_per_capita.png)

Between 2012-13 and 2013-14, both per-capita figures fell slightly — the only period in the dataset where per-capita spending declined — before the nine-year data gap.

## The Inflation Caveat

All figures here are nominal. Canada's CPI rose approximately 35–40% between 2012 and 2025. Real per-capita gains, while still positive, are more modest than the nominal figures suggest.

For K-12 specifically, the nominal per-capita gain of ~22.5% likely represents a near-flat or slight real-terms decline after inflation adjustment. Post-secondary per capita roughly doubled in nominal terms (+78%), suggesting meaningful real gains even after inflation — though how much of that reflects increased enrolment, increased cost per student, or both requires enrolment data not examined here.

## Summary

| Finding | Detail |
|---|---|
| Total spending increase (2012–2025) | +$7,483M (+83%) nominal |
| Fastest-growing sector | Post-secondary at +132% |
| Population growth over same period | +30.5% |
| Per-capita K-12 change | $1,617 → $1,981 (+22.5%) |
| Per-capita post-secondary change | $747 → $1,330 (+78.0%) |
| Post-secondary budget share | Grew from ~32% to ~40% of total |
| Inflation caveat | Nominal figures; CPI rose ~35–40% over the period |

Alberta clearly chose to grow its education budget substantially faster than its population — particularly in post-secondary. Whether this represents a genuine increase in educational capacity, catch-up from underfunding in the intervening years, or a response to demographic shifts in the student-age population cannot be determined from spending data alone.
