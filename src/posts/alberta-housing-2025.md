<div class="abstract">
<p>In 2025, Alberta recorded nearly 54,900 housing starts — a record going back at least seventy years. Seven in ten were multi-unit dwellings. Despite making up less than 12% of Canada's population, the province accounted for nearly a quarter of all national housing starts. Calgary and Edmonton both achieved record annual construction volumes. Yet the resale market told a different story: Calgary's annual benchmark price fell 2% to $577,492, total sales dropped 16%, and inventory rose sharply as new listings surged 9%. Edmonton's sales declined 5.8% to 29,050 units, while detached home prices continued climbing. The divergence between record construction and softening demand defines the central tension of Alberta's 2025 housing market — a market caught between a construction boom built on yesterday's migration surge and an economic reality shaped by slowing population growth and rising uncertainty.</p>
</div>

## Record Construction, Record Supply

Alberta's construction sector operated at a pace unseen in the province's modern history. Nearly 54,900 housing starts were recorded in 2025, an increase of more than 7,000 over 2024 and a record stretching back at least seventy years.[^1] Seven in ten of those starts were multi-unit dwellings — rowhouses, apartment buildings, and stacked townhomes — reflecting a structural shift in what Alberta builds.

| Metric | 2025 | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Total housing starts | ~54,900 | Record high (70+ years) |
| Increase over 2024 | +7,000 units | ~14% year-over-year |
| Multi-unit share | ~70% | Rowhouses + apartments |
| Multi-unit starts | ~38,600 | Record, up from ~31,400 in 2024 |
| Rental starts | ~20,000 | Strong rental construction |
| Alberta's share of national starts | ~25% | Despite 12% population share |

Calgary drove much of the activity. In the first six months alone, the city recorded 11,178 housing starts — a 38% increase over the same period in 2024. Calgary posted the highest number of housing starts per 10,000 residents of any major Canadian market, approaching a record high. Edmonton matched the intensity, recording an 82% year-over-year increase in starts during June.[^2]

The scale of Alberta's construction relative to its population is striking. The province houses roughly one in eight Canadians but built roughly one in four of the country's new homes in 2025. This disproportionate output reflects what ATB Financial's chief economist described as "catch-up" activity — construction responding to the migration wave of 2022–2024 that added approximately 598,000 people to Alberta between 2021 and 2025.

## Calgary's Resale Market: The Correction Arrives

While builders broke ground at record rates, Calgary's resale market cooled materially. The Calgary Real Estate Board's year-end data tells the story of a market that shifted from seller-driven conditions to something closer to balance.

| Metric | 2025 | Year-over-Year Change |
|---|---|---|
| Total residential sales | 22,751 | -16% |
| New listings | 40,000+ | +9% |
| Annual benchmark price (all types) | $577,492 | -2% |

The decline in sales was broad-based but uneven across property types. Apartment condominiums were hit hardest, falling 28% year-over-year — though CREB noted volumes remained 28% above long-term historical trends, suggesting the decline was from an elevated peak rather than a collapse. Row homes fell 17%. Detached and semi-detached segments held up better, declining 9% and 8% respectively.[^3]

| Property Type | Benchmark Price | Year-over-Year Change |
|---|---|---|
| Detached | $752,767 | +1% |
| Semi-detached | $685,850 | +3% |
| Row homes | — | -2% |
| Apartment condos | — | -3% |

The price data reveals a clear split. Higher-priced segments — detached and semi-detached homes — continued appreciating modestly, supported by constrained supply and persistent demand from higher-income buyers. Lower-priced segments — row homes and condos — declined as new supply from the construction boom and slowing migration reduced competition for entry-level units.

This divergence matters. Alberta's post-pandemic migration wave was disproportionately composed of younger workers and immigrants who entered the market at the lower end. As that demographic pressure eased in 2025, the segments most exposed to migration-driven demand were the first to soften.

## Edmonton: Quieter Decline, Persistent Affordability

Edmonton's market followed a similar trajectory but with a key structural difference: prices started from a much lower base, and affordability remained a distinguishing feature.

| Metric | 2025 | Year-over-Year Change |
|---|---|---|
| Total residential sales | 29,050 | -5.8% |
| December average price (all types) | $454,981 | +4.7% |
| MLS HPI composite benchmark (Dec.) | $415,300 | +2.8% |

Edmonton's 5.8% decline in annual sales was less severe than Calgary's 16% drop, and the city's benchmark price actually rose 2.8% year-over-year by December. However, the headline masks significant variation by property type.

| Property Type | December 2025 Avg. Price | Year-over-Year Change |
|---|---|---|
| Detached | $566,552 | +5.2% |
| Semi-detached | $422,078 | +3.3% |
| Row/townhouse | $297,124 | +1.6% |
| Apartment condo | $193,577 | -5.2% |

The pattern mirrors Calgary: detached homes gained ground while condos declined. But the magnitude of Edmonton's affordability advantage is stark. A detached home in Edmonton averaged $566,552 — roughly $186,000 less than Calgary's detached benchmark of $752,767. Edmonton's average apartment condo price of $193,577 was nearly half of Calgary's apartment pricing. This gap has made Edmonton an increasingly attractive destination for interprovincial migrants priced out of both Calgary and the broader national market.[^4]

Inventory told its own story. Edmonton's year-over-year inventory rose 28.9% by December, signalling that the supply side was catching up to demand — and in some segments, overtaking it.

## The Rental Market: Supply Finally Arrives

Alberta's rental market in 2025 marked a turning point. After years of tight conditions, a wave of purpose-built rental completions began reshaping the landscape.

Calgary's purpose-built rental supply grew 11% in 2025 — the fastest expansion in decades. Despite that surge, the vacancy rate held at approximately 5%, suggesting robust demand absorbed much of the new supply. However, rent growth stalled. Landlords held two-bedroom rents steady to retain tenants and avoid vacancies in an increasingly competitive market.[^5]

Edmonton's rental market loosened further. Purpose-built rental vacancies rose to 3.8%, driven by strong completions and slower household formation. Over 2,000 rental condos entered the market in 2025, pushing the share of condo apartments in the rental universe to 37%. Landlords offered incentives to fill units — a reversal from the tight conditions of 2023 and early 2024.

| City | Vacancy Rate (2025) | Avg. 1BR Rent (June 2025) |
|---|---|---|
| Calgary | ~5.0% | $1,537 |
| Edmonton | ~3.8% | ~$1,302 |

The $235 monthly gap between Calgary and Edmonton one-bedroom rents reflects the same affordability differential visible in the ownership market. For renters, Edmonton remained materially cheaper — a factor likely to influence migration patterns in the years ahead.

## Population Growth: The Engine Decelerates

The construction boom of 2025 was built on the assumption that Alberta's extraordinary population surge would continue. It did not — at least not at the same pace.

Alberta's population growth slowed through each quarter of 2025. After years of quarterly growth rates regularly exceeding 1.0%, the province recorded 0.4% growth in Q1, 0.38% in Q2, and just 0.2% in Q3 — the lowest quarterly rate since Q2 2021. Over the first three quarters, Alberta added approximately 52,690 people, reaching an estimated 5,029,346 by mid-year.[^6]

| Quarter | Growth Rate | Approx. Population Added |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 2025 | 0.4% | ~20,000 |
| Q2 2025 | 0.38% | ~19,268 |
| Q3 2025 | 0.2% | ~11,500 |

Even at these reduced levels, Alberta stood alone among provinces. In Q3 2025, Alberta and Nunavut were the only jurisdictions in Canada to record population growth — every other province and territory saw its population decline, driven by federal immigration policy changes that sharply reduced non-permanent resident inflows.

But the deceleration matters for the housing market. A province adding 50,000 people per year requires materially fewer new homes than one adding 150,000 to 180,000, as Alberta was doing in 2022–2024. The 54,900 housing starts of 2025 were calibrated for the old pace. If population growth continues to moderate, the gap between supply and demand will narrow — and in some segments, may already have reversed.

## The Central Tension

Alberta's 2025 housing market presents an apparent paradox: record construction alongside falling prices and declining sales. The resolution lies in timing.

Housing construction operates on long lead times. The record starts of 2025 reflect decisions made in 2023 and 2024, when Alberta was experiencing the steepest population surge in its modern history and demand for housing — rental and ownership alike — was running well ahead of supply. Builders responded rationally to those conditions. By the time those units broke ground, the migration wave had already begun to ebb.

The resale market, by contrast, responds to current conditions. Buyers in 2025 faced slowing population growth, rising inventory, economic uncertainty from trade tensions, and a market psychologically recalibrating from the frenzy of 2022–2024. The result was predictable: sales volumes fell, price growth stalled or reversed in lower-priced segments, and the market shifted toward balance.[^7]

Several questions remain unanswered by the 2025 data alone. How much of the construction pipeline is pre-sold versus speculative? What share of new rental supply is absorbed by in-migration versus existing residents upgrading? Will the federal immigration slowdown persist, or will Alberta's relative economic strength attract a larger share of a reduced national intake? And perhaps most consequentially: will builders adjust their pace, or will starts remain elevated into 2026 even as demand conditions soften?

The data examined here establishes the trajectory. Alberta built at an extraordinary rate in 2025. The market told builders to slow down. Whether they listened will define 2026.
